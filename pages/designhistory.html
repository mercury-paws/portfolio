History of Design

The history of design spans centuries and reflects humanity's evolving relationship with function, form, and beauty. From ancient symbolic patterns to modern minimalism, design has been a visual language used to communicate values, status, and innovation. Each era brought distinctive styles influenced by culture, technology, and social change.

As societies grew more complex, design developed from craft-based ornamentation into a powerful discipline shaping everything from everyday objects to architectural landscapes.

Repetitive Design Patterns

Repetition in design creates harmony, rhythm, and continuity. Across civilizations, recurring motifs have symbolized beliefs, structured compositions, and enhanced aesthetics. These patterns, often deeply rooted in cultural identity, transcend time through their balanced, symmetrical arrangements.

Let's explore six influential styles that utilize repetition uniquely:

Victorian

Victorian design emerged during Queen Victoria's reign (1837-1901) and emphasized opulence and intricate detail. Repetitive floral patterns, damasks, and ornate scrollwork reflected wealth and sophistication. The era favored symmetry and layering of patterns in wallpapers, textiles, and furnishings.

Art Deco

Art Deco, popular in the 1920s-30s, embraced geometric repetition, bold lines, and metallics. It was a style of luxury and modernity, often featuring sunbursts, chevrons, and stepped forms. Repetition added elegance and futuristic flair to buildings, interiors, and fashion.

Bauhaus

The Bauhaus school, founded in 1919 in Germany, championed simplicity and function. Repetition of basic geometric shapes—circles, triangles, squares—defined its minimalist aesthetic. This approach made design accessible, unifying art, craft, and industry.

Aztec

Aztec design is recognized by its bold, symmetrical motifs and symbolic imagery. Repeated zigzags, step-fret shapes, and stylized animals conveyed spiritual meaning and cosmic order. These patterns adorned textiles, temples, and ceramics, expressing power and belief.

Islamic

Islamic design is known for its infinite geometric patterns, tessellations, and arabesques. Avoiding figurative imagery, it celebrates divine unity through complex repetition. These motifs are found in mosques, tiles, and manuscripts, often using interlaced stars and polygons.

Japanese

Traditional Japanese patterns, or *wagara*, incorporate natural themes like waves, clouds, and chrysanthemums. These motifs, repeated in elegant rhythms, reflect harmony and simplicity. Repetition in Japanese design emphasizes balance, seasonality, and a connection to nature.

Sistine Madonna

  • Title: Sistine Madonna
  • Artist: Raphael (Raffaello Sanzio da Urbino)
  • Date: c. 1512
  • Medium: Oil on canvas
  • Dimensions: Approximately 265 cm x 196 cm
  • Current location: Gemäldegalerie Alte Meister, Dresden, Germany
  • Type of work: Painting

What drew me to this work was precisely the emotional ambiguity. The Madonna is not idealized to the point of detachment; rather, she is human. Her expression reminds me of moments of quiet dread before a major life change. It connects with the idea of knowing something painful is coming and still choosing to go forward. This painting made me think of religious icons, but also of modern depictions of motherhood that show vulnerability rather than perfection. The cherubs at the bottom add a touch of innocence and contrast, but I can't help but feel they emphasize the solemnity above them. I believe Sistine Madonna holds a place in the museum not just for its artistic mastery but for its emotional intelligence. It says something universal through religious imagery.

The Chocolate Girl

  • Title: The Chocolate Girl (German: Das Schokoladenmädchen)
  • Artist: Jean-Étienne Liotard
  • Date: c. 1743-1745
  • Medium: Pastel on parchment, mounted on canvas
  • Dimensions: Approx. 82 cm x 52 cm
  • Current location: Gemäldegalerie Alte Meister, Dresden, Germany
  • Type of work: Pastel painting

What drew me to this painting is its ability to make the ordinary seem extraordinary. She is not a saint, a mythological figure, or royalty—she's a servant, possibly delivering a breakfast tray. Yet, she's painted with dignity and care, as if Liotard deeply admired her presence. It reminded me of the modern appreciation for everyday beauty, like candid street photography or film stills that capture silent moments. There's no drama here, no high symbolism—just an honest portrayal of grace in routine. I think this painting is in a museum because it shows technical brilliance in pastel (a challenging medium) and also shifts the focus of art from nobility and myth to the realism and respect of human labor and character. It invites us to notice and value what is usually overlooked.

The Starry Night

  • Title: The Starry Night
  • Artist: Vincent van Gogh
  • Date: 1889
  • Medium: Oil on canvas
  • Dimensions: 73.7 cm x 92.1 cm
  • Current location: The Museum of Modern Art (MoMA), New York City, USA
  • Type of work: Painting

What drew me in was the feeling of the sky moving—like something eternal and unknowable pressing down over something small and still. The painting reminds me of nights when thoughts race and sleep won't come, when the stars feel close enough to touch but distant all the same. The cypress tree reminded me of a flame or a reaching hand—it feels symbolic, maybe of longing or connection. I also saw a kind of hope in the glowing orbs of stars, a belief that beauty and wonder persist even in mental turmoil. This painting feels deeply personal, as if we're seeing the world not just through Van Gogh's eyes, but through his emotions. It's in the museum not only because of its fame and beauty, but because it captures the rawness of human feeling—grief, awe, isolation, and imagination—all in one moment.

The Poor Party

  • Title: Poor Party
  • Artist: John Valadez
  • Date: 1985
  • Medium: Pastel on paper
  • Dimensions: Approx. 48 × 60 inches (varies depending on exhibition)
  • Current location: Various private and public collections; often exhibited in Chicano art retrospectives and museum shows
  • Type of work: Pastel drawing

What drew me to Poor Party was how it honors a scene that might otherwise go unnoticed or dismissed. It reminds me of neighborhoods I've passed by or lived in—where people gather in parking lots, yards, or street corners late at night to share music, drinks, and life, not because they have luxury, but because they have each other. There's something so honest and unfiltered in how the people are shown. They're not idealized or judged; they just are. The work makes me think about how art often leaves out working-class stories unless they're romanticized or politicized—Valadez does neither. He just presents people as they are, beautifully. I believe Poor Party is exhibited in galleries and museums because it reframes what is considered 'museum-worthy.' It brings Chicano culture, everyday struggle, and community pride into the center of the art world. It's not just technically brilliant—it's socially vital.

Pompidou

  • Title: L'uvre (Work inspired by/related to Centre Pompidou)
  • Artist/Architect: Aldo Loris Rossi
  • Date: Late 20th century
  • Medium: Architectural models, urban planning proposals, conceptual drawings
  • Context: Italian architect influenced by radical architecture; work often discussed in the context of Centre Pompidou's high-tech, inside-out style
  • Location: Exhibitions at Centre Pompidou, or in academic/architectural archives
  • Type of work: Architectural proposal/drawing/model

I was drawn to this work because it confused me. At first I couldn't tell if it was a sculpture or a real building. Then I realized that's exactly the point—it blurs the boundary between function and form, art and infrastructure. It reminded me of the Centre Pompidou itself, which famously wears its mechanical systems on the outside. But Rossi's approach felt more chaotic, even mythic—like a city built after a revolution, or in a post-human future. This made me think about the cities we live in now—how sterile or lifeless they can be. Rossi's L'uvre proposes something more daring: cities that breathe, stretch, mutate. It's not just a design—it's an argument. I believe it's shown in institutions like the Centre Pompidou because it challenges our expectations of architecture. It's not comfortable. It's a manifesto in physical form.

The Climate Stripes

  • Title: The Climate Stripes
  • Artist/Scientist: Ed Hawkins (University of Reading)
  • Date: 2018
  • Medium: Digital visualization
  • Type of work: Data-driven visual art / infographic

This piece caught my attention because it doesn't 'look' like art or science at first glance. It's both, and neither. I was struck by how quickly it made me feel something. It didn't tell me anything—it showed me. It reminded me of Alvin Lustig's modernist graphic designs—how he could suggest complex emotions with just shape and tone. Like Lustig, Hawkins embraces abstraction to deliver clarity. There's also something of Mark Rothko in the way the bands of color speak without words—meditative but also unsettling. I believe this work belongs in museums, galleries, classrooms, and public spaces because it redefines how data can be felt, not just understood. It's a visual siren: elegant, immediate, and unforgettable.
